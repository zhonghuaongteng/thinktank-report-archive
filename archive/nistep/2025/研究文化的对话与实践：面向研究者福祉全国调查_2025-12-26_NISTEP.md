# 研究文化的对话与实践：面向研究者福祉全国调查

## 中文摘要与研判

该页面汇集 NISTEP 讨论论文《研究文化的对话与实践》及相关报告、演讲材料和国际参考文献，并说明其将在 2026 财年开展面向日本研究者福祉与研究文化的全国调查。材料将论文数、引文数、经费额等可量化产出之外的“研究土壤”作为研究能力的前置条件，强调好奇心、失败学习、代际隐性知识、安全提问关系、研究评价改革和研究者主观体验。对中国和上海的科研体系建设而言，该材料的价值在于把科技创新支撑从投入和产出指标推进到研究文化、评价机制、人才生态和 AI 时代科研方式变化的制度层面。

This page brings together the reports and presentation materials relating to these efforts, together with materials on relevant international developments. Rethinking — and observing — research from the soil that sustains its roots The number of papers, citation counts, the research funding secured — for a long time, the achievements of research have been measured by figures such as these. They are, without doubt, important indicators. Yet what do those figures reflect about the act of research, and what do they leave unseen? The state that researchers themselves — the very people who produce these achievements — are in right now is a question that figures struggle to capture. The true strength of research most likely lies upstream of those numbers. A pure impulse to know; the persistence to learn from failure; the tacit knowledge handed down across generations; and relationships in which one can pose questions with a sense of security — such “invisible values” quietly sustain research. Between the entrance of research investment and the exit of published papers lies a domain that is hard to see. We call it “research culture,” and we are seeking to recast it as the “soil” that nurtures research capabilities. The conditions for shedding light on this soil are now coming together. An international foundation for measuring subjective well-being with reliability and validity has been established; a movement to question research assessment anew from the standpoint of “people and culture” is spreading worldwide; and generative AI has begun to transform the very shape of research at speed. Japan, too, has placed the recovery of research capabilities among its most important challenges. It has become possible to observe; the world is changing direction; and the object of observation is itself moving rapidly right now — we stand at this intersection. To address this question head-on, the NISTEP Wellbeing and Research Culture Team (established February 2026) is, this fiscal year, preparing to conduct a “Nationwide Survey of Researchers on Wellbeing and Research Culture.” It will be the starting point of an attempt — one with few precedents in Japan or abroad — to make visible, with reliability and validity, researchers’ subjective experience and the research culture that sustains it. What we have gathered here are materials for thinking through this question together: records of the dialogues NISTEP has held with researchers and people of diverse standpoints; theories and methods for making research culture and wellbeing visible; and the work of international organizations and consortia looking in the same direction — the OECD (Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development), AGORRA (A Global Observatory of Responsible Research Assessment), CoARA (Coalition for Advancing Research Assessment), and DORA (San Francisco Declaration on Research Assessment). From the Japanese-language report to an English digest and records of lectures and symposia, all can be traced here. How do researchers living well and the creativity of science relate to one another? There is as yet no definitive answer to this question. That is precisely why we believe it is important to deepen our thinking together, through dialogue with people of diverse standpoints. Please explore the materials below according to your interests. Japanese-language report ■ DISCUSSION PAPER No.244 “Research Culture in Dialogue and Practice: From the NISTEP Co-Creation Workshop and Public Symposium to a Theoretical Framework and Empirical Studies on the ‘Soil that Nurtures Research Capabilities’” (published December 2025) Link: https://nistep.repo.nii.ac.jp/records/2000276 (DOI: https://doi.org/10.15108/dp244 ) Produced as part of the NISTEP TEITEN Survey (the regular expert survey on Japan’s S&T and innovation system), this report aims to recast research capabilities not merely as “outputs” but from the perspective of the “soil” (research culture) that nurtures them (published 26 December 2025). Building on the NISTEP Co-Creation Workshop of 22 November 2024 and the public online symposium of 20 December 2024, it compiles the results of dialogue among diverse actors — researchers, university research administrators (URAs), funding-agency (FA) professionals, and policymakers. It organizes the three key concepts drawn from the dialogue — “latent (unexpressed) value,” the “internal evaluation system,” and the “sandbox” — and proposes a “six-dimensional analytical framework” for capturing research culture in a multifaceted way. Overall moderator: Tomoko Sakai (Senior Researcher, NISTEP) https://youtu.be/Plk3OYsowGQ NISTEP-related report ■ STI Horizon — “Constructing a Multidimensional Indicator System to Enhance the Well-being of Researchers in Society” (Tomoko Sakai; Vol.11, No.3, 2025) Link: https://doi.org/10.15108/stih.00413 An article introducing the concept of a NISTEP exploratory project launched in April 2025 (published September 2025). Seeking to move away from uniform evaluation centered on paper counts and citation counts and to build a new indicator system that reflects the diverse values and conceptions of happiness held by researchers, it sets out the development of a multidimensional indicator system that integrates insights from well-being studies, neuroscience, the mathematical sciences, and policy science, among other fields. Its theoretical foundations lie in Aristotle’s eudaimonia and VanderWeele’s theory of human flourishing. Building on these, it proposes a five-dimensional indicator for measuring “researcher flourishing” — subjective well-being in one’s research; physical and mental health; sense of mission and purpose in research; academic integrity and collaboration; and connection to, and support within, the research community — together with a “participatory indicator design” in which researchers themselves can choose the axes by which they are evaluated. ■ STI Horizon — “‘Research Culture’ as the Soil that Nurtures Research Capabilities: From the Philosophy and Practice of the Human Frontier Science Program (HFSP)” (Hidemi Tomita, Masatsura Igami, Tomoko Sakai; Vol.11, No.4, 2025) Link: https://doi.org/10.15108/stih.00419 An article that, through the case of the international and interdisciplinary research funding program HFSP, analyzes the nature of a “research culture” that nurtures research capabilities (published December 2025). It identifies as success factors a review process that does not require preliminary data and prizes innovativeness, mechanisms for collaboration among researchers across fields and countries, and an environment in which early-career researchers can take on challenges without fear of failure; and it traces the background to HFSP having produced 31 Nobel laureates among its research-grant recipients in the 35 years since its founding. Going back to the etymological root of “culture” as “cultivating the mind,” it argues that what underpins these institutional designs is the philosophy and culture shared among those involved, and suggests that cultivating a research culture — one that encompasses researchers’ values and modes of behavior, not merely the pursuit of quantitative indicators — is indispensable to strengthening Japan’s research capabilities. OECD (Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development) — Reference Materials ■ OECD, “Reforming research assessment for better science” (OECD Policy Briefs, No.56, 2026) Link: https://doi.org/10.1787/f6202159-en A policy brief on research assessment reform. Recognizing that excessive reliance on narrow performance metrics undervalues collaboration, openness, societal impact, and the like and generates distorted incentives, it offers recommendations urging governments to position research assessment reform within national strategies and policies and to incorporate alignment with emerging international principles into the criteria for institutional assessment. It is the summary-and-recommendations version of the working paper below. ■ OECD, “New expectations and demands from science: Rethinking research assessment frameworks” (OECD Science, Technology and Industry Working Papers, No. 2026/07, 2026) Link: https://dx.doi.org/10.1787/0c685800-en The detailed analytical version that underpins the brief above. Surveying research assessment at the system level, it organizes the principal tensions, actors, and drivers, and, through a comparative review of the literature, sets out common principles for reform. It treats the interrelationships among research assessment, incentives, and research culture. ■ OECD, “OECD Guidelines on Measuring Subjective Well-being (2025 Update)” (2025) Link: https://doi.org/10.1787/9203632a-en The second edition of the international guidelines on measuring subjective well-being (a revision of the 2013 first edition). Based on the three components of life evaluation, affect, and eudaimonia (meaning and purpose), it introduces three principal changes: a short core module comprising three priority indicators; streamlined extended modules for each component; and new cross-cutting and experimental modules covering hitherto unmeasured concepts. AGORRA (A Global Observatory of Responsible Research Assessment) — Reference Materials A frontier attempt to observe research culture and research environment internationally — one example of overseas work that shares the problem awareness of this initiative. ■ RoRI (Research on Research Institute), “Indicators of Research Culture and Environment” (Cameron Neylon, RoRI Working Paper No.19, May 2026) Link: https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.32237928 A working paper examining indicators of research culture and environment as part of AGORRA, RoRI’s international observatory project on research assessment. Conducted in parallel with the UK REF’s “People, Culture and Environment (PCE)” pilot, it documents how the terms “research culture” and “research environment” are used, depending on context, to denote different issues — research integrity, diversity and inclusion of talent, resource allocation, career structures, researcher mobility, external engagement, and so on. Noting that many existing indicators are skewed toward two sources — organizational reporting requirements (such as Athena Swan) and research outputs — it draws on Ostrom’s Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework to interrelate indicators from the perspectives of organization and community and to make gaps visible. It concludes that neither a single indicator nor a “basket” of indicators can fully capture the complexity of research culture and environment, and that what matters is whether organizations and communities can productively receive the feedback from monitoring and evaluation, and whether indicators can be adapted, changed, or retired as needed. CoARA (Coalition for Advancing Research Assessment) — Reference Materials An international framework through which the world’s research institutions and funding agencies advance research assessment reform in solidarity — embodying, at the broadest scale, the “move away from an excessive emphasis on outputs” that this initiative shares. ■ CoARA, “Agreement on Reforming Research Assessment” (July 2022) Link: https://www.coara.org/agreement/the-agreement-full-text/ The foundational agreement of CoARA. Released in July 2022, it was compiled through co-creation involving more than 350 organizations across over 40 countries. Calling for a move away from excessive reliance on paper counts, citation counts, and the journal impact factor, and for the qualitative evaluation of the diverse outputs, practices, and activities of research, it sets out ten core commitments. Signatory organizations become members of CoARA and advance research assessment reform based on the agreement’s principles while learning from one another. More than 800 organizations worldwide now participate. In recent years, locally rooted venues for dialogue have also been spreading. In June 2026, a regional forum was held in Budapest, and, in parallel, an event on research assessment reform in the Asia-Pacific region was held in Singapore, co-hosted by CoARA, DORA, and ALLEA (as part of the Force11 conference). DORA (San Francisco Declaration on Research Assessment) — Reference Materials The longest-standing international declaration and initiative, and the starting point of research assessment reform — among the first to confront head-on the “excessive reliance on output metrics” that this initiative questions. ■ DORA (San Francisco Declaration on Research Assessment) Link: https://sfdora.org/ A declaration released in 2013 that criticizes the practice of using the journal impact factor as a measure of an individual researcher’s performance. Calling for improvement in how research and researchers are assessed, it continues today, as a global initiative to that end, to support implementation at institutions and to collaborate with other organizations. DORA is currently advancing the formulation of its strategic plan for 2027–2030, and as part of this it gathered views widely through an international survey running through April 2026. The survey aimed to learn what is working well in research assessment reform, what challenges still remain, and what kind of support from DORA would be useful in advancing “responsible research assessment” across diverse contexts. The views received will be reflected in deliberations during 2026, and the next strategic plan is scheduled to be released in early 2027. * The Wellbeing and Research Culture Team was established in February 2026 (Reiwa 8) as the Research Culture Team and was renamed to its current name in April of the same year.
