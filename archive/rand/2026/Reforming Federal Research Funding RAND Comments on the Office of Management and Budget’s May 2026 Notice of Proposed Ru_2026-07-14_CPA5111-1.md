# 改革联邦科研资助：兰德对美国行政管理和预算局2026年5月联邦财政援助规则修订草案的意见

## 中文摘要与研判

### 核心观点

该意见书评估美国行政管理和预算局2026年5月拟将联邦财政援助指南改为约束性规则并调整资助管理制度的运营后果，重点面向接受联邦科研资助的机构。兰德对部分减负措施持支持态度，但总体采取明确警示立场，认为若未修改，若干条款将增加行政浪费、降低透明度、破坏科研资助可预期性并形成无法回收的合规成本。其分析基于兰德78年管理联邦科研资助的经验，围绕新增合规负担、资助全生命周期与择优机制、允许成本规则歧义三类问题展开。意见书指出，声誉风险监测条款可能迫使机构持续追踪分包方的公开言论、媒体报道和人员社交媒体活动，却没有范围、触发条件或终止点；逐笔付款说明要求也与既有预算、进度报告和审计机制重复。兰德估算，一个管理约70项资助的研究机构仅首年就可能为付款说明增加约2730小时劳动，而规则影响分析却未量化这一成本。文件承认延长多年期项目预算周期等改变能够减少负担，其反对重点是缺乏成本证据、标准含混和取消既有程序保障的条款。

### 建议

兰德建议删除开放式声誉风险监测义务；若保留，应定义重大声誉损害并限于分包任务履约。对于常规付款，应允许已批准预算满足说明要求，仅在明显偏离预算或进度时追加报告；对自由裁量终止、成本规则和申诉程序，也应保留可预期性、费用补偿及透明的择优和复核机制。

Reforming Federal Research Funding RAND Comments on the Office of Management and Budget’s May 2026 Notice of Proposed Rulemaking Revising the Guidance for Federal Financial Assistance CP-A5111-1 July 2026 Corporate Publication ii About This Comment In May 2026, a notice of proposed rulemaking entitled “Regulation for Federal Financial Assistance” was published in the Federal Register by the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) and many other federal agencies.1 This document presents RAND’s comment on the proposed changes to 2 CFR,2 which governs the administration of federal financial assistance awards across the federal government, including grants, cooperative agreements, and other forms of assistance. These changes fall into several broad categories: redefining the legal status of OMB’s guidance from advisory to binding regulation that federal agencies must follow; restricting the categories of topics and activities eligible for federal funding; revising cost principles that determine what expenditures recipients may charge to federal awards; and modifying the processes governing how awards are made, managed, suspended, and terminated. Many of the comments that OMB has received on the rulemaking address the proposed shift from guidance to controlling regulation, legal questions, and the new restrictions on fundable topics and activities. This comment addresses the operational implications of the proposed rule for research organizations, because these implications are significant and may be underrepresented in other public comments. RAND is a nonprofit, nonpartisan research organization committed to the public interest. This comment draws on 78 years of experience managing federal research awards in compliance with federal grant award regulations. It was developed by Andrew R. Morral and Marjory S. [200.332(i)] Pass-Through Entity Consultation on Subrecipient Reputational Actions This provision requires pass-through entities to consult with federal agencies when a subrecipient takes “actions that could significantly damage” the reputations of the pass-through entity, the agency, or the government. The standard is unclear and would impose new and burdensome oversight and monitoring procedures, as well as frequent legal analysis by both federal agencies and pass-through entities to determine whether a given subrecipient action triggers the consultation obligation. This will almost certainly increase government-reimbursable overhead costs (a subset of indirect costs) for pass- through entities. The RIA does not quantify these costs. “Significantly damage” as it relates to reputations is undefined, the range of subrecipient activities that could be characterized as damaging is wide, and the rule appears to apply to any action taken by subrecipients, not just those specific to their subaward. A subrecipient that experiences a data breach, becomes the subject of negative media coverage, faces an enforcement action by a state regulator, or is named in litigation could, under some reading, “significantly damage” the reputation of the government. A subrecipient with a staff member who becomes the subject of an enforcement action, adverse media coverage, or public controversy unrelated to the funded work could fall within the provision’s reach. The standard contains no limiting principle distinguishing ordinary institutional activity and routine operational challenges from actionable reputational harm. Because the obligation to consult arises when any action “could significantly damage” reputations—not when it actually does—pass-through entities must make a forward-looking judgment about reputational risk under an undefined standard. Current subrecipient monitoring requirements focus on grant performance: financial compliance, expenditure of funds consistent with approved budgets, and delivery of agreed-upon work products. The § 200.332(i) consultation obligation would require something different and more comprehensive: continuous monitoring of a subrecipient’s public profile, statements, and activities outside the scope of grant performance. To fulfill this obligation, pass-through entities would need to track subrecipients’ publications, public statements, media coverage, advocacy activities, litigation posture, and potentially staff social media activity, all in addition to evaluating whether the subrecipient is performing the funded work. For organizations that manage large portfolios of subrecipient relationships, this represents a significant and open-ended monitoring burden with no defined scope, trigger threshold for consultation, or termination point. 11 The RIA does not acknowledge or quantify this cost, which would be substantial if such extensive monitoring were even feasible. Recommendation: OMB should remove § 200.332(i) from the rule. [200.305(c)(2)] Payment Justification Requirements The proposed rule would require every payment request from recipients and subrecipients—other than states—to include a written justification describing the purpose of the payment and the specific award-related work it supports. OMB describes this change as increasing “accountability for Federal disbursements” consistent with Executive Order 14222 but provides no empirical basis for concluding that existing accountability mechanisms are inadequate or that payment-level justifications would improve them. 12 Federal awards are already subject to extensive oversight at every stage of performance. Recipients must maintain internal financial controls and are subject to annual Single Audit requirements above the expenditure threshold. They submit progress reports—quarterly, semi-annual, or annual, depending on award terms—that document milestone completion and link expenditures to activities. Recipients are subject to monitoring visits, post-award audits, and cost allowability reviews. These mechanisms already condition disbursement on demonstrated performance and give federal agencies a clear picture of how funds are being used. Given the extensive existing oversight framework, it is not clear that the marginal improvement in oversight offered by payment justifications would offset the organizational costs that would be incurred. Good evidence bearing on these questions should be available, because payment justification was implemented in 2025 as part of the government’s Defend the Spend program. OMB could justify this rule change by demonstrating that Defend the Spend produced measurable reductions in improper payments or meaningfully improved identification of fraud at agencies that implemented demanding versions of this requirement such as proposed in § 200.305(c)(2) (e.g., the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, the Administration for Children and Families, and the Department of State) compared with rates at agencies that implemented only pro forma justification requirements. Although that evidence should be available, the RIA provides no such analysis. For a research organization managing dozens of active awards with biweekly payment cycles, this requirement would add substantial administrative labor annually. For an organization managing about 70 awards, we estimate that approximately 2,730 hours in additional labor would be required in the first year. The RIA describes the costs of this change as “modest” without quantitative support. Research organizations would invest in new systems and processes that would reduce this burden over time but could not eliminate it entirely. Rule Changes That Harm Award Life-Cycle Predictability and Merit-Based Selection The proposed changes to termination, suspension, and appeals create serious reliance interest concerns, impose unquantified real costs on recipients when awards are suspended or canceled, and remove administrative safeguards that help the government and recipients avoid waste of public funds. Several provisions also introduce undefined compliance criteria that will cause research organizations to adopt risk-averse proposal policies that avoid topics that could conceivably trigger undefined compliance thresholds, depriving the government of proposals and work that are in its interest to fund. [200.340(a)(2)] Discretionary Termination of Awards OMB characterizes discretionary termination as a clarification of existing authorities and therefore anticipates little or no cost associated with this change. For the purposes of this comment, we treat this provision as a new authority. Discretionary termination is a standard tool in federal procurement, where the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) allows agencies to realign contract work with changing needs. But whereas the proposed rule introduces a termination-for-convenience framework like that found in the FAR, it discards protections available through the FAR that ensure equitable terms for both parties. Under the FAR, for instance, contractors may submit termination settlement proposals to recover costs incurred, and they retain full appeal rights under the Contract Disputes Act to an administrative board or the Court of Federal Claims. The RIA characterizes discretionary terminations as having only transfer effects, not costs. In the research assistance context, termination imposes costs on both parties. The government has paid for work that will often produce no findings, data, or other public benefit. The knowledge that a study was designed to produce often does not exist in partial form: A policing effectiveness study canceled before outcomes data are collected cannot provide any effectiveness information even if 80 percent of its budget has been expended. Most returns on research investment are realized at completion or not at all. Recipients and subrecipients also bear unrecoverable costs that the RIA does not acknowledge. Research organizations make employment commitments to staff hired on specific projects— commitments that cannot be immediately reversed when an award is terminated. Staff whose time is charged to terminated projects cannot always be transferred to other ongoing work, and employment agreements may obligate organizations to provide a notice period before laying off such staff. During that period, the organization incurs overhead costs that cannot be recovered against the terminated awards. Because the timeline between developing proposals aligned with changed government priorities and eventual award of new work is often many months to over a year, staff support through these transitions can require prolonged and costly overhead spending. These costs are borne by the research institution, not the federal government, even though they result directly from federal termination decisions. Because decisions about laying off staff or covering their time on overhead raise difficult 8 trade-offs, abrupt terminations impose significant management burdens on leadership and senior administrators, which is another significant organizational cost. There are often project wind-down expenses that, under the proposed rule, could not be recovered, which would then become an institutional expense. Research projects involving human participants present many challenges: A longitudinal survey with hundreds of participants cannot responsibly shut down without first contacting participants, explaining to them that the project ended prematurely, and informing them what will happen with the data they have already provided. This ethical obligation to participants exists regardless of whether funding continues. Subawards, consulting agreements, software licenses, and many other procurement contracts must be canceled.
