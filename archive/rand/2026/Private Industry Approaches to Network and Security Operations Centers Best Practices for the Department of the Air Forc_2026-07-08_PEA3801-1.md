# 私营部门网络与安全运营中心经验：美国空军可借鉴的做法

## 中文摘要与研判

### 核心观点

这篇 RAND 论文讨论的是私营企业如何组织网络运营中心和安全运营中心，以及这些经验如何供美国空军现代化指挥控制网络时借鉴。作者的核心判断偏向务实支持：私营部门在 NOC/SOC 结构、人员分层、外包和自动化方面形成了可迁移做法，能帮助国防部门在保障网络性能和安全的同时降低人力压力。文章指出，NOC 通常聚焦业务关键系统的可用性和性能，依靠低层级分析员、自动化平台和逐级升级机制处理例行故障；SOC 则负责日志、告警、入侵、恶意软件和内部滥用监测，并以检测时间、事件处置速度和合规结果衡量绩效。主要证据包括，企业常通过托管服务商或托管安全服务商获得 24/7 覆盖，内部团队保留事件响应、取证和高层沟通等关键职责。文章还提醒，SIEM、SOAR 等自动化工具可以缓解人员短缺，但过度依赖工具或调校不足会制造安全盲点。内部争论在于，外包和自动化能提升效率，却可能削弱对关键任务和复杂事件的直接控制；作者强调应在精简人力和保留核心响应能力之间取得平衡。

SOC Team Analysts, by Organization Size ................................................................................................... 7 vi 1 Private Industry Approaches to Network and Security Operations Centers The U.S. Department of War (DOW) began developing the concept of Combined Joint All Domain Command and Control (CJADC2) in 2019 as an effort to improve command and control by connecting systems across the land, air, sea, space, and cyberspace domains.1 The Advanced Battle Management System (ABMS) division within the Department of the Air Force’s (DAF’s) Program Executive Office for Command, Control, Communications and Battle Management funds the development of architecture and systems engineering, digital infrastructure (DI), software and applications, and aerial networking required for the DAF’s role in CJADC2. ABMS Digital Infrastructure (ABMS-DI) is a key component of this effort, developing a suite of fixed and deployable DI solutions that provide lightweight and scalable secure processing, connectivity, and data management for critical command and control functions. This new network infrastructure must be both efficient and resilient, requiring dedicated personnel and resources to manage the infrastructure, troubleshoot problems, and provide cybersecurity. The DAF has proposed standing up a command- and-control combined network and security operations center (C2NSOC) to support DAF operational elements using ABMS and ensure that ABMS performance and security meet the demands of CJADC2. In contrast to the DAF, which focuses on mission objectives, private industry is looking to engage customers and maintain services that sustain revenue, and it depends on DI to do so. Organizations generally operate two core functions to maintain digital reliability and security: These are a Network Operations Center (NOC) and a Security Operations Center (SOC), respectively. This paper details common approaches used by NOC and SOC organizations in private industry, highlighting (1) which practices could improve efficiency in the DAF C2NSOC and (2) situations in which a different approach might be needed. We survey both NOC and SOC functions and responsibilities, organizational structures commonly used by private companies, and the corresponding manpower and staffing requirements. We also describe how advances in cybersecurity technology might reduce staffing requirements if properly implemented, and we conclude with lessons learned that the DAF could apply as the C2NSOC and ABMS-DI mature. 1 The concept was originally Joint All Domain Command and Control (JADC2). The Department of War is designated the Department of Defense under Public Law 81-216, National Security Act Amendments of 1949. 2 Functions and Responsibilities The NOC ensures network availability and performance across on-premises, cloud, and remote environments. The SOC safeguards corporate assets from cyber threats that could disrupt operations, compromise data, or damage brand reputation. Private-sector NOCs are primarily tasked with ensuring that services remain available to customers and employees. NOC analysts continuously monitor switches, routers, application servers, and cloud services for outages or performance degradation. Performance is usually measured in terms of network uptime, throughput, and latency, which directly affect availability of critical business services. Network disruptions can also expose the business to financial risk. When disruptions occur, NOC teams restore service through root-cause analysis and coordination with infrastructure and vendor support. Cost-efficiency strongly shapes the private industry NOC. Staffing models often rely on lower-tier analysts supported by automation platforms; only problems that exceed routine troubleshooting are escalated to senior engineers. In efforts to reduce redundancy and optimize the use of potentially limited numbers of staff, many firms also consolidate NOC operations across business units or outsource them to managed service providers (MSPs) to reduce overhead while maintaining coverage 24 hours per day, 7 days a week (24x7). 2 In-house NOCs in private industry are therefore often as lean as possible, focusing tightly on availability and performance of business-critical systems.3 Private-industry SOCs focus on protecting data, intellectual property, and customer trust. SOC analysts monitor logs and alerts from across the enterprise to detect intrusions, malware, and insider misuse. SOC performance is evaluated against detection times, incident resolution speed, and compliance outcomes. For firms in regulated industries—such as finance, health care, or retail—SOC operations also must demonstrate compliance with regulatory and industry requirements, such as the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard, the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 in the United States, and the General Data Protection Regulation in the European Union. 4 Budget realities influence private-industry SOC operations similar to the ways those realities affect NOCs. Many companies adopt hybrid models in which routine alert monitoring is outsourced to managed security service providers (MSSPs), while in-house teams retain control of incident response, forensic investigations, and communication with executives. Private firms also are increasingly using automation platforms—such as security information and event management (SIEM) platforms and security orchestration, automation, and response (SOAR) tools—to compensate for staff shortages, 5 although overreliance on tools and inadequate tuning can create blind spots in the security system and create opportunities for cyber threats. The dividing line between NOC and SOC responsibilities might not be as clear cut in practice as we have outlined. Lack of coordination in such incidents risks extended downtime and increased financial loss. Change management is another shared domain. Both NOC and SOC staff must ensure that when patches or configuration updates are deployed, these changes do not degrade service or open new vulnerabilities. Without adequate coordination, a regular configuration update that causes a temporary service outage might be interpreted by SOC personnel as a security incident. 8 Reporting responsibilities also overlap, though the emphasis differs. The NOC provides leadership with metrics on uptime and service availability; the SOC reports on compliance posture and risk reduction. A centralized model is one in which a single NOC or SOC handles operations for the entire firm. The NOC or SOC in this model usually operates from in a single physical location and might share space with other corporate information technology (IT) functions. 10 This approach is common in industries in which economies of scale are important and network infrastructure can be monitored effectively from one location. According to the 2025 SANS SOC survey, the centralized model is still the most common, used in more than one-third (38 percent) of private firms. 11 Centralized operations reduce duplication of effort and simplify management oversight, but they also create potential single points of failure. 12 A localized disaster or system outage at the central facility could significantly hinder the ability to monitor and respond to incidents. Furthermore, a single-center approach can complicate responsiveness across global enterprises in which employees and customers span multiple time zones. Nonetheless, centralization remains attractive for many private firms because it is cost-efficient and provides the ability to maintain consistent procedures and toolsets across the enterprise. A distributed model, in contrast, establishes multiple NOC or SOC facilities across regions or business units. This approach usually entails a higher cost to the organization but enables coverage across geographies and time zones, can more easily support local regulatory requirements, 13 and enhances responsiveness in the event of a local facility outage. Distributed operations are common in multinational corporations with diverse business lines or strict regional compliance obligations, such as those in financial services and health care. However, distributed centers can also introduce coordination challenges and potential inefficiencies.
