# 高带宽内存是什么，为什么重要

## 中文摘要与研判

### 核心观点

这篇 RAND Expert Insights 解释高带宽内存 HBM 的技术含义及其对 AI 的战略重要性。作者的核心判断是明确肯定性的：HBM 已成为领先 AI 芯片和高性能计算硬件的关键使能技术，因为大模型需要同时具备高存储容量和高内存带宽。文章指出，HBM 通过堆叠 DRAM die，在接近处理器的位置存放模型权重和上下文数据，能够满足传统 DRAM、SRAM 等单一技术难以同时满足的容量和速度需求。主要证据包括，2026 年大多数领先 AI 芯片使用 HBM，几乎所有领先 AI 模型都在搭载 HBM 的 AI 芯片上训练和部署；HBM 生产需要先进光刻、垂直堆叠和极高精度，光刻设备成本可超过 3 亿美元。文章还指出，HBM 可能占 AI 芯片总生产成本 45%，约为处理器制造成本的三倍，显示其不仅是技术部件，也是成本和供应链瓶颈。内部争论在于，外界常把 AI 芯片竞争聚焦于 GPU 或处理器，作者则强调内存容量、带宽和封装制造同样决定 AI 能力边界。

### 建议

文章面向研究者和政策制定者提出，应把 HBM 生态系统纳入 AI 硬件和出口管制分析，不能只跟踪处理器本身。

### 中国/上海参考

中国参考在于，文章指出全球竞争性 HBM 生产主要由 SK Hynix、三星和美光承担，产能集中在韩国、台湾和日本；长鑫存储正在扩大国产 HBM，但总体产出和最先进代际仍落后。由于 2024 年 12 月后中国被禁止进口国外 HBM，中国先进 HBM 获取能力取决于 CXMT 能否进一步提升。

PILZ, NICHOLAS BROWN, LENNART HEIM High Bandwidth Memory What It Is and Why It Matters H igh Bandwidth Memory (HBM) is a type of computer memory used in leading artificial intelligence (AI) chips and other hardware for high-performance computing. HBM can store and move large volumes of data quickly, making it suitable for increasingly data- intensive AI workloads. 1 As of 2026, most leading AI chips use HBM, and almost all leading AI models are trained and deployed on AI chips with HBM. 2 In the following section, we explain what sets HBM apart from other memory technologies and why HBM is important for AI’s computational needs. KEY TAKEAWAYS ■ Leading artificial intelligence (AI) workloads place extreme demands on memory, and Hi gh Bandwidth Memory (HBM) has emerged as the central technology to meet them. Large AI models require chips with both high memory capacity —how much data can be stored—and high memory bandwidth —how fast data can be transferred to a processor. HBM is the only memory technology that meets both requirements: It offers higher bandwidth than other types of dynamic random-access memory (DRAM) while providing enough storage capacity to hold model weights and contextual data close to the processor, which is some- thing that smaller, faster memory, such as static random-access memory, cannot do on its own. This combination is why most of the top AI chips use HBM. ■ HBM p roduction is a complex, capital-intensive process that demands some of the world’s most advanced manufacturing equipment. First, companies fabricate individual DRAM memory dies —unpackaged silicon chips—at large production facilities. Each die requires etching nanometer-scale features using lithography tools that can cost more than $300 million. The DRAM dies are then stacked vertically, which requires extreme preci- sion because a single defect can compromise the entire HBM stack. HBM’s complexity and resource-intensiveness make it expensive to produce: It can account for 45 percent of the total production cost of an AI chip, roughly three times the cost of fabricating the chip’s processor. ■ Onl y three companies—SK Hynix (South Korea), Samsung (South Korea), and Micron (United States)—produce globally competitive HBM. Their production is concentrated in South Korea, Taiwan, and Japan. Chinese company CXMT (ChangXin Memory Technologies) is attempting to increase its domestic HBM production but lags in overall output and does not manufacture the most-advanced HBM generations. In December 2024, China was barred from importing HBM from foreign producers, so Chinese access to advanced HBM depends on whether CXMT can improve. Expert Insights PERSPECTIVE ON A TIMELY POLICY ISSUE July 2026 2 High Bandwidth Memory in the Broader Memory Landscape The ability to store and access data is central to com- puting, but different workloads have varying require- ments for how much memory is needed and how quickly they need to access that memory. Computer systems thus use a hierarchy of memory technolo- gies that fall into two broad categories. The first is volatile memory: It requires constant power to retain data and serves as a system’s active working memory, holding the data and instructions that running pro- cesses need. 3 The second is non-volatile memory, which retains data without power and is used for long-term storage. 4 In each category, there are mul- tiple subtypes of memories that offer different trade- offs in access speed, capacity, and cost. In practice, a single device, whether a laptop or a server, relies on several of these technologies at once, each handling a different role. At one end of the spectrum are fast and expensive volatile memories with low capacity, such as static random-access memory (SRAM). 5 SRAM is built into processors, such as central processing units (CPUs) or graphics processing units (GPUs), and provides data near-instantaneously, but it has low capacity. At the other end are non-volatile storage types that are slow and cheap and offer high capacity. These include hard disk drives (HDDs) or solid-state drives (SSDs): They can hold large amounts of data affordably, but accessing that data takes a long time. Dynamic random-access memory (DRAM) occupies a middle ground in capacity, bandwidth, and access speed (Figure 1). It is a volatile memory that has higher bandwidth and higher access speed than SSDs or HDDs and offers much greater capacity at a lower cost than SRAM. 6 It comes in several vari- ants optimized for different applications. Double data rate (DDR) DRAM is the standard, general-purpose variant, used in servers and personal computers; LPDDR (low-power DDR) is designed for energy efficiency in phones and laptops; and GDDR (graph- ics DDR) is optimized for graphics processing and offers higher bandwidth than standard DDR. HBM is the most specialized form of DRAM, designed to meet the extreme memory demands of leading AI workloads. Unlike conventional DRAM modules that place dies side by side on a circuit board, HBM stacks multiple DRAM dies vertically and places them next to the processor, providing greater memory capacity and higher data transfer rates in a smaller footprint. Leading AI workloads rely on specialized AI chips designed by such companies as Google, Nvidia, and Advanced Micro Devices (AMD). 7 These chips deliver high processing speeds, but two things are required from memory to make effective use of that computational power: first, the ability to keep large amounts of data close to the processor, and second, high bandwidth—the rate at which data can move between memory and the processor. Both AI training and inference require sub- stantial memory capacity close to the processor. AI chips must hold a model’s parameters (the numerical weights that determine the output of the model) in working memory so that the processor can access them quickly. Large AI models contain hundreds of billions of parameters, requiring hundreds of gigabytes (GBs) of memory just to hold the weights alone. 8 For example, a 500-billion-parameter model in FP16 precision would need approximately 1 tera- byte (TB) of memory, so much that the model needs to be distributed across at least six Nvidia B200 AI chips, each carrying 192 GB of HBM3E. 9 Alterna- tively, the same model would need at least hundreds of SRAM-based chips to run because of their lower capacity per chip. Relying on long-term storage devices, such as HDDs or SSDs, would be impracti- cal because they are orders of magnitude slower and cannot deliver data quickly enough to keep work- loads running. Model weights are not the only data that must reside in working memory. Modern AI models based on the transformer architecture also maintain what is known as a key-value (KV) cache. The KV cache func- tions like a notepad for everything the model has read so far in a given request so that the model does not have to reprocess the entire input each time it gener- ates the next word. As the amount of input data a model can process (also known as the context window) has grown up to a million tokens, the KV cache has grown accordingly, consuming an increasing share of available memory alongside the model weights. 10 Importantly, each concurrent user requires their own separate KV cache, leading to memory demands that scale along with the size of the user base. 11 Keeping all this data in memory is necessary but not sufficient: The processor also needs to read it fast enough to avoid sitting idle. During training and inference, massive volumes of data flow between memory and the AI chip’s processor. If the data is not delivered fast enough, the processor stalls and the computing power goes unused. 12 In practice, much of the time spent generating new tokens during inference is not limited by how fast the processor can compute but by how fast it can read from memory. For those reasons, it is a memory-bound workload. This is why memory bandwidth is so critical for AI performance. HBM provides a balance of specifications that meet AI’s memory needs. It delivers far higher band- width than other types of DRAM, such as DDR or LPDDR, while offering enough dense capacity close to the processor to hold model weights and KV cache—something that on-chip SRAM, despite being faster, cannot provide on its own because of its limited capacity. No other single memory technol- ogy matches this combination. That said, as model sizes and context windows continue to grow, AI chip designers are actively adopting and exploring trade- offs across the memory hierarchy. These include allocating more die area to SRAM, pairing HBM with high-capacity DDR for less frequently accessed data, using SRAM-based chips alongside HBM-based designs for parts of inference, or developing new HBM provides a balance of specifications that meet AI’s memory needs. At the time of this writing in early 2026, HBM is the centerpiece, but the broader memory architecture around it continues to evolve. High Bandwidth Memory Design HBM’s combination of high memory bandwidth and capacity makes it stand out from other memory alter- natives, and both metrics have improved substan- tially across generations. The first generation of HBM debuted in 2013 and offered 128 GB per second of bandwidth and 4 GB of capacity per stack. 13 HBM4, whose standard was finalized in 2025, delivers up to 2 TB per second of bandwidth and 64 GB of capacity per stack, increases of around 16 times for each. 14 A typical AI chip contains multiple stacks of HBM (Figure 2).15 For example, Nvidia’s B300, released in 2025, uses eight stacks of HBM3E. Each of the HBM stacks uses 12 DRAM dies and a base die, enabling the chip to reach 288 GB of total capacity and 8 TB per second of bandwidth. 16 Two design features enable HBM to reach these performance specifications: vertical stacking and wide interfaces that can transmit high volumes of data in parallel. We explain both in detail in the fol- lowing sections. Vertical Stacking A core feature of HBM is its vertical architecture: Multiple DRAM dies are vertically stacked (Figure 2) and thus substantially increase the amount of data HBM can store in a compact package. Microscopic vertical channels (TSVs) route data to and from individual dies in the stack at high throughput. 17 At the bottom of the stack is a base logic die (base die), which orchestrates the rapid transfer of data between memory and the processor of the AI chip. How Is High Bandwidth Memory Produced, and What Makes It So Challenging? In the previous section, we explained HBM’s place in the broader memory landscape, why it is useful for leading AI workloads, and its main design features. In this section, we provide an overview of HBM’s manufacturing processes, which can be divided into three technically challenging domains, each consist- ing of many substeps: DRAM wafer fabrication, base die fabrication, and die stacking. DRAM Wafer Fabrication The HBM manufacturing process begins at DRAM fabrication facilities, known as fabs for short (Figure 3, Step 1). Here, manufacturers transform raw silicon wafers into functional memory dies through a series of steps that deposit, pattern, and remove materials on a nanometer scale. The fabrication pro- cess requires large clean rooms that span the size of multiple football fields and are outfitted with billions of dollars in specialized tools, including lithography machines—produced exclusively by the Dutch firm ASML—that can cost more than $300 million. 22 As of 2026, front-end DRAM production for HBM is primarily in South Korea, Taiwan, and Japan, and there is more-limited capacity for lagging generations in China. 23 The United States does not have facilities that produce leading-edge DRAM for HBM at scale. A key metric that determines a manufacturer’s DRAM sophistication is called the manufacturing node. 24 In semiconductor manufacturing, this metric measures the smallest feature size in nanometers (nm) that can be printed on a silicon wafer. 25 Smaller features are an important improvement because they increase memory cell density, allowing more bits (and thus more data) to be stored per unit of silicon area. 26 Importantly, these features also enable reductions in heat generation and energy consump- tion. Since producers started making DRAM with feature sizes below 20 nm in 2016, they have named nodes using the number “1” combined with a lettered progression (x, y, z, α, β, γ). 27 The big three memory manufacturers—Samsung, Micron, and SK Hynix— started volume production of 1z process nodes with feature sizes of around 16 nm between 2019 and 2020 (Figure 4). 28 As of 2026, they have advanced to feature sizes approaching 10 nm. Meanwhile, the most-advanced DRAM that Chinese memory manu- facturer CXMT (ChangXin Memory Technologies) has produced at commercial scale in 2026 has a fea- ture size of 16 nm, putting its technology around four years behind the industry’s state of the art. 29 Advances in DRAM are crucial for new HBM generations because improvements in DRAM density While HBM is well suited for AI, it has significant trade-offs, particularly its high production costs and manufacturing complexity. 6 help to unlock higher bandwidth and stack capaci- ty.30 Improvements also drive cost reductions for fabs, increasing the number of gigabytes that can be produced per wafer. However, migrating to more- advanced DRAM nodes is difficult and takes time: Manufacturers need to reoptimize certain process steps and introduce new materials and equipment. 31 As a result, manufacturing yields —the percentage of dies that are functional on a silicon wafer—are lower when a new node is introduced but improve over time as problems are uncovered and resolved. 32 Unlike other types of memory dies, HBM requires DRAM with an extra step—the addition of TSVs used to route data through the dies of the verti- cal stack. 33 This increases the resource intensiveness and difficulty of front-end manufacturing, for two main reasons. First, the DRAM dies used in HBM are larger and less dense than those in conventional DRAM because of the inclusion of TSVs. 34 As a result, in HBM, achieving the same memory capac- ity (in gigabytes) requires using significantly more silicon wafers—roughly three times as many as are needed for DDR5. 35 Second, forming the TSVs is a substantial technical challenge. 36 Each TSV requires drilling microscopic holes through the silicon, lining the holes with insulating material, and filling them with conducting metal; defects can occur at any of these steps. 37 TSV manufacturing is getting more challenging as HBM makers try to shrink the size of TSVs to preserve space for data storage. 38 FIGURE 3 High Bandwidth Memory Manufacturing Process Flow NOTE: Step 1: DRAM fabs transform raw silicon wafers into DRAM with TSVs in steps that deposit, pattern, and remove materials on a nanometer scale. Base Die Fabrication HBM requires a base die to manage data transfers between the stacked DRAM dies and the AI chip’s processor (Figure 3, Step 2). For HBM generations up to HBM3E, base dies for HBM were exclusively produced in house on DRAM process nodes. How- ever, memory manufacturers are starting to migrate their base die fabrication to dedicated logic found- ries, either at Samsung or Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC). 40 Moving base die fabrication to logic foundries will contribute to bandwidth and power efficiency gains, making the base die increasingly important for overall HBM per- formance. 41 As of HBM4, base dies are increasingly customized to meet the design and performance objectives of a customer’s processor. 42 They can be tuned to different priorities depending on the use case; for example, a power-constrained edge deploy- ment might sacrifice bandwidth for a smaller, more efficient footprint, while a large-scale training cluster would optimize for maximum bandwidth and capac- ity regardless of power or size. 43 Base die fabrication is difficult because it com- bines the complexity and tool requirements of advanced logic with unique memory constraints.
